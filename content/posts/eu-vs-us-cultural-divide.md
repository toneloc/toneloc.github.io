Here in early 2026, there is a growing rift between Europe and the United States. Bruno Maçães attributes this to a growing Europhobia from US conservatives, triggered and exacerbated by megalomaniac Donald Trump's efforts to acquire Greenland. But I see this as a decades-long trend, and, as is often the case, it took Trump to say the quiet part out loud. 

Europeans and Americans are really different. Most notably to me, Americans are much more religious. They have more children—the US fertility rate stands at 1.6 births per woman, while the EU hit a historic low of 1.38 in 2023. Americans are also risk takers. Europeans ... simply aren't.

In late 2011 and 2012 I spent several months as an intern in Brussels, Belgium when I was in graduate school for economics. They have a special name for these interns in Brussels, <i>stagiaire</i>. It was an interesting time to be there. 


Two episodes from that time crystallized this for me. I recall that when they occurred, I thought to myself, "Europe has no shot; this place is stagnant." The first episode happened immediately when I began my first internship at a lobbying firm.
